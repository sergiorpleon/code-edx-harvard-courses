History of Chess

The predecessor of all games in the chess family, it presumably arose in India northern as a game for four. This primitive chess would be known as chaturanga in Persia and after The conquest by the Arabs would continue to develop following the Islamic expansions.

The Arabs conquered the Sassanid empire between 632 and 651. During that time they entered contact with chess. Through them came the game, which only by phonetic adaptation is called shatranj, to his first era of great splendor. The Arabs contributed decisively to its expansion.

The game entered Europe through several routes. One of the first contacts occurred through Byzantine Empire, especially Constantinople. The Varangians brought the game from Constantinople (Miklagard, for them) to Russia, where it began to be played from the beginning of the 8th century. Through the Arabs brought chess to Spain in the 9th century. The oldest European text, which contains the rules of the game, probably comes from between the years 900 and 950 and from northern Italy.

Towards the end of the 15th century they began to change the rules decisively. They would regulate movements of the pawn, the bishop and the queen. The pawn could now take two steps on the first move (until then one only); the bishop, at any distance (until then it only jumped two squares); and the lady, at her discretion in any of the eight directions (previously called alferza, it advanced only one square in the direction diagonal), suddenly going from being a relatively weak figure to becoming the strongest of the board and to provide great spectacularity to the game.

Through these adjustments the game was changed completely, the birth of modern chess. The new game It required different tactics and openings. The exercise gained speed and, at the same time, popularity. These innovations were probably introduced in Valencia between the years 1470 and 1490 and were manifested in he Valencian poem Scachs d'amor, the oldest document on modern chess. The composers and Once famous chess players were Francesc de Castellvi, Narcis Vinyoles and Bernat Fenollar. In the year 1495, Francesch Vicent published the first chess book with the title Libre dels Jochs Partits dels Schacs in Name of 100 sort and compost. Currently there is no copy of this incunabulum, although there are reasons to believe that some specimen should be preserved, in fact a juicy reward is offered to whoever find one. In 2005, the historian José Antonio Garzón (Chelva, 1966) through the study of some manuscripts that are preserved in Italy managed to recreate the book, a fact that he captured in his book The Return by Francesch Vicent: The story of the birth and expansion of modern chess.

The tradition of organizing chess competitions began in the 16th century and the first official championship of the world of chess was organized in 1886.

Currently, the International Chess Federation is the highest authority on chess as a sport and the international organization that establishes the universally accepted rules of chess.

The strength of chess players ranges from those who barely know how to move the pieces and the who are capable of playing games essentially error-free and with an astonishing depth of ideas. For players advanced, the Elo score has been established, which allows us to get an idea of ​​how good the player.

This is perhaps the most widely accepted classification of players according to their Elo score:

Elo Description
From 0 to 1400 points: Beginner Level. These players have little experience and their game is characterized for showing numerous obvious errors that are easily detectable by a regular player during the game. departure.
Between 1400 and 1600 points: Amateur level or average club player. At this level the players already show that they have certain knowledge about tactics and strategy of the chess, although these Knowledge has been mostly acquired through own experience obtained during the games. and not for a Rigorous study of the game. Federations or clubs usually assign an initial Elo score of 1400 to players who enter their ladder for the first time.
Between 1600 and 2100 Elo points: Semi-professional or strong club player level. This range covers the vast majority of non-professional club players. Players at this level develop correct chess where serious mistakes are rare. Club players usually have read one or more chess books and are very experienced in all phases of the game. They also usually attend competitions and train and try to improve their game regularly.
Between 2100 and 2300 Elo points: Candidate Master (CM). This is the first of the ranks recognized by FIDE, although it is not an official title. In many countries, federations recognize players with an Elo rating of 2200 or higher with the title of National Master (NM).
Between 2300 and 2400 Elo points: FIDE Master (FM). Since 1978, FIDE recognizes as Masters players who reach an Elo rating of 2300. In November 2010, there are approximately 5,731 active players with the rank of FIDE Master.
Between 2400 and 2500 Elo points: International Master (IM). Created in 1950 along with the title of Grandmaster (GM), this title recognizes players whose playing strength allows them to compete at the same level with players from other countries. Although these titles are obtained through specific results in tournaments, the International Master can be awarded in other specific ways, for example, to the World Junior Champion, or to someone who achieves the performance (expected rating) of a 2400 Elo player in a Chess Olympiad. There are currently 3,258 active players with the title of International Master.
Elo from 2500 points: Grandmaster (GM). Created in 1950, this title is, after the title of World Champion, the highest recognition for the best chess players. Currently, about 1419 players have the rank of Grandmaster. The youngest player to reach the rank of Grandmaster was the Indian-American Abhimanyu Mishra, at 12 years, 4 months and 25 days of age, in 2021​.

The use of systematic searches to design computer chess programs began with the work of Claude Elwood Shannon. However, we could cite as a precursor the Spanish engineer Leonardo Torres Quevedo, who designed a finite state automaton capable of playing king and rook endgames.

It was not until the 1970s and 1980s that advances in computing power and improved programs led to a great improvement in computer play. Soon they began to beat even grandmasters in rapid games and later in ordinary tournament conditions. This advance culminated in the defeat of world champion Garry Kasparov by IBM's supercomputer Deep Blue in 1997, with the score 3.5-2.5. More recently, in November 2006, the Deep Fritz program, running simply on a personal computer with an Intel Core 2 Duo processor, also managed to defeat then world champion Vladimir Kramnik by a score of 4-2.

In December 2017, Alphazero outperformed Stockfish and other top chess programs. Unlike other programs, AlphaZero, the artificial intelligence created by Deep Mind, owned by Google since 2014, is not based on human knowledge. Its understanding of chess, beyond the basic rules, comes solely from its ability to self-learn. After playing almost five million games for four hours against itself, AlphaZero acquired the same knowledge as humans have in almost fourteen hundred years.
